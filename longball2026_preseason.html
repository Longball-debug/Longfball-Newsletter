Commissioner's Letter

Eighteen Seasons. Seven Champions.

Since the first pitch of 2008, LONGBALL has been more than a fantasy baseball league. It has been an arena where dynasties rise, underdogs shock the world, and every March brings the hopeful fiction that this year — this year — is finally the year. What follows is the complete recorded history of all 14 franchises, active and alumni, verified through the 2025 season.

Seven teams have hoisted the championship banner across 18 seasons. The Arizona Desert Rats stand alone atop the all-time record books with five titles, but the last five seasons have seen three different champions — a sign that the league's balance of power is shifting. Goldy Goldy Gone, just five seasons old, are your reigning 2025 champions. Before the current era, the Houghton Lake Bleacher Bums — owned by Mark Sheehan — became LONGBALL's first back-to-back champions in 2010–2011, defeating the Ithaca Spartans both times. Their legacy is permanent. Welcome to 2026.

Arizona Desert Rats 232–110 · .678
5 Championships 2009 · 2016 · 2017 · 2018 · 2020 Est. 2008 · Mesa, AZ

No franchise in LONGBALL history has dominated the way the Desert Rats have. Five championships, six conference titles, a .678 all-time winning percentage, and 232 wins. The numbers alone tell the story of a dynasty — but the details make it legendary.

Their three-peat from 2016–2018 is the defining stretch of the modern era: a 15–6 title, followed by 17–4 and 18–3 back-to-back league and conference doubles. Then came 2020 — a 17–1 record that stands as the single most dominant season in LONGBALL history. The 2020 Desert Rats lost exactly one game all year.

The last two seasons (8–9 in 2024, 9–8 in 2025) represent the longest championship drought in franchise history. But the Rats have rebuilt before, and they enter 2026 at 232–110 all-time with every reason to believe the dynasty isn't finished.

"17–1. The most dominant season in LONGBALL history. That's the standard this franchise set."

Midland Red Weasel Dogs 169–88 · .658
3 Championships 2012 · 2019 · 2023 · 2024 Est. 2012 · Midland, MI

Four championships. The only franchise that has genuinely challenged the Desert Rats for league supremacy. The Red Weasel Dogs arrived in 2012 going 14–4 and winning it all immediately, and they haven't stopped competing since.

Their back-to-back titles in 2023 and 2024 — including a 14–3 regular season record in the championship year — cemented their status as the league's premier modern dynasty. The 2025 season brought another 14–3 finish and another regular season crown, only for the championship to slip away to Goldy Goldy Gone. Three straight years with the best record in the league is extraordinary.

At 169–88 and a .658 winning percentage, the Dogs enter 2026 as one of the most dangerous teams in the league, motivated by a playoff loss that stings. When the Dogs are hungry, the rest of the league should be worried.

"Back-to-back-to-back with the best regular season record — and four rings. The Red Weasel Dogs are LONGBALL's modern dynasty."

Goldy Goldy Gone 54–29–1 · .649
1 Championship 2025 Reigning Champions Est. 2021 · Never Had a Losing Season

Five seasons in, Goldy Goldy Gone have won a championship, never posted a losing record, and now carry the highest active winning percentage in the league at .649. The most exciting young franchise in LONGBALL is also, suddenly, the most accomplished.

Their 2025 championship run — a 13–4 regular season record for the second straight year — completed a remarkable rise. From a 6th-place debut in 2021, to back-to-back 13–4 campaigns in 2024 and 2025, to a title banner, Goldy Goldy Gone have built something real and fast.

As defending champions, the target is on their back in 2026. Every team will be scheming to knock them off. The question is whether this franchise can do what the great ones do: sustain it. Based on five seasons of evidence, the answer appears to be yes.

"Five seasons. Five winning records. One championship. Zero losing seasons. The league's newest dynasty is already underway."

Newberry Indians 178–120 · .597
3 Championships 2014 · 2015 · 2021 Est. 2010 · Newberry, MI

Three championships, five conference titles, a .597 career winning percentage, and consecutive near-misses in 2024 and 2025. The Indians have been one of the most consistently excellent teams in LONGBALL history — and heading into 2026, they're hungry for a fourth ring.

The franchise reinvention from their rocky early years (7–14 in 2010, 6–12 in 2012) to back-to-back champions in 2014–2015 is one of the great rebuild stories in league history. A third title followed in 2021. Since then, the Indians have been a top-four fixture: runner-up in 2024, 3rd in 2025, with 12 wins each time.

At 178–120 with a core that is clearly still championship-caliber, the Indians enter 2026 as legitimate title contenders. Unfinished business is the theme.

Troutdale Cascades 202–140 · .591

The original LONGBALL champions. The Cascades won the 2008 title in the league's very first season and have been chasing ring #2 for 17 years since. At 202–140 all-time, they've crossed 200 franchise wins and remain one of the most consistently competitive organizations in the league.

Five conference titles across their history — in 2012, 2013, 2016, 2019, and 2021 — prove they can peak with the best teams. The 2025 season brought their most exciting playoff run in years: a 9–8 regular season somehow turned into a run all the way to the championship game, falling just short as runner-up.

That near-miss in 2025 should fuel the Cascades entering 2026. The second ring has been 17 years in the making. Based on recent form, it feels closer than ever.

Ithaca Spartans 144–197–1 · .423
1 Championship 2013 Est. 2008 · Ithaca, MI Back-to-Back Last Place 2024–25

The most contradictory franchise in LONGBALL. An original member since 2008, a league champion in 2013, and the team that absorbed the most heartbreak in league history — losing the championship to the Bleacher Bums in both 2010 and 2011, finishing runner-up before finally breaking through in 2013.

Since that title, the Spartans have been maddeningly inconsistent. Conference championships in 2014 and 2022 show the talent is real. But 4–17 in 2018, 4–13 in 2023, 3–14 in 2024, and 2–15 in 2025 — four of the worst seasons in league history — paint a franchise in freefall. Back-to-back last-place finishes entering 2026 mean top draft capital two years running.

At 144–197–1 all-time, the losses now outnumber the wins by 53. But this league has seen dramatic rebounds before — and the Spartans, with premium draft picks in hand, have all the pieces for a turnaround. The 2026 season will be the first real test of whether the rebuild is real.

Fourteen seasons and a .339 winning percentage. The Scorpions have been the league's longest rebuild project — a franchise that has never found a formula to sustain the promise shown in their 2012 and 2013 debut seasons (back-to-back 10–8 records, 4th and 6th place).

The mystery of the Scorpions is that their fantasy point totals have consistently outpaced their win column — this team scores more than you'd expect from a team with their record. The wins just aren't there. A change in approach entering 2026 could unlock what has been a frustratingly dormant franchise.

Houghton Lake Bleacher Bums 31–10 · .756
2 Championships 2010 · 2011 Houghton Lake, MI · Owner: Mark Sheehan Alumni — Departed

Before anyone else had figured out what kind of league LONGBALL was going to be, Mark Sheehan's Houghton Lake Bleacher Bums went out and defined it. Back-to-back championships in 2010 and 2011 made the Bums the first — and still only — team in league history to win consecutive titles.

The 2010 championship (14–7 regular season) came with a decisive victory over the Ithaca Spartans in the final, introducing a rivalry that the Spartans would desperately try to avenge. In 2011, the Bums went 17–3 — one of the most dominant regular seasons in league history — and then survived the most dramatic championship game LONGBALL has ever seen, holding off the Spartans by exactly half a point to repeat.

The Bleacher Bums eventually departed the league, leaving behind a two-ring legacy, a .756 all-time winning percentage, and a standard no franchise has matched since. Wherever Mark Sheehan is, he holds a permanent place in LONGBALL history. The first back-to-back champion. Won by half a point.

"The first back-to-back champions in LONGBALL history. Won the second title by half a point. The Bleacher Bums legend is permanent."
