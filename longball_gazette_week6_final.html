Week 6 Final — May 4–10, 2026
CASCADES UNSTOPPABLE; BANK STUNS GOLDY; SCORPIONS PULL THE UPSET NOBODY SAW COMING
Six weeks in, the Troutdale Cascades are a runaway freight train with no brakes and no apologies. The BANK of Texas fires their two best arms in the same week and finally topple Goldy Goldy Gone. The Tempe Scorpions win the ugliest, most satisfying game of the week. And the Sons of Pitches post 188 points, which isn’t a score — it’s a distress signal.
By the Longball Gazette Wire Staff
League Headquarters — Sunday, May 10, 2026

The Troutdale Cascades posted 504.167 points in Week 6. They are now 5-1. They have posted 500-plus points twice this season. They have the highest cumulative point total in MLBLONGBALL at 2,835.5 — three hundred and fourteen points clear of second-place Goldy Goldy Gone. At some point this stops being a hot streak and starts being a statement about who this team is. We are at that point. Jacob Misiorowski (36.5 pts, 0.00 ERA) anchored yet another dominant pitching performance as the Cascades dispatched Sidd Finch — who managed 260 points for the second time in three weeks — by a margin of 244 points. When a team your size loses by 244, the problem is structural, not circumstantial.

The upset of the week — and arguably the upset of the young season — did not come from an obvious source. The BANK of Texas, a franchise that has spent six weeks searching for consistency, walked into Goldy Goldy Gone’s house and walked out with a 365-340 victory. Cristopher Sanchez was extraordinary: 69.5 points, 15 innings, 1.80 ERA. Davis Martin matched him pitch for pitch at 61.5 points over 13.2 innings at a 0.75 ERA. Two starters, one team, one week: 131 combined pitching points. Goldy had answers — Cody Bellinger went 9-for-24 for 46.5 points, the week’s second-highest hitter total — but not enough of them. The BANK wins 3-3 and suddenly looks like a team that figured something out.

In the most unexpected result of the week, the Tempe Scorpions — sitting at 2-4 entering play, their season a quiet accumulation of close losses and bad draws — took down the Ithaca Spartans 330 to 274. Eduardo Rodriguez (59.17 pts) and Michael Wacha (50 pts) gave the Scorpions their best two-ace outing of the season. Miguel Vargas hit his way to 40 points and a 6.67 FP/G pace that nobody in this building predicted when the season started. The Spartans, meanwhile, fall to 1-5 — a record that, given the talent on that roster, defies easy explanation and demands a harder conversation before Week 7.

The Cascades Are Who We Thought They Were — Better

Five wins, one loss, 2,835.5 cumulative points. The Troutdale Cascades have not just won — they have won consistently, deeply, and without an obvious ceiling in sight. Their Week 6 output against Sidd Finch was a demolition job: 504 points against 260, a margin that would be embarrassing if the Cascades weren’t simply the most complete roster in this league right now. The question heading into Week 7 against the Sons of Pitches is not whether they win. It is by how much, and whether the Sons survive it with their season still technically alive.

The BANK Finally Fires on All Cylinders

Let the record show that when the BANK of Texas deploys Cristopher Sanchez and Davis Martin in the same week, they are capable of beating anyone in this league. One hundred and thirty-one pitching points from two starters is not a rotation — it is a weapon. The problem the BANK has faced all season is the inconsistency of the arms around those two, and the quiet failure to maximize their position player depth. Week 6 was proof of concept. Now comes the hard part: doing it again in Week 7 against the Tempe Scorpions, who just showed they can score too.

It is also the most important: it proves the roster is capable of beating the best team in the league when fully deployed.

The Tempe Scorpions have spent six weeks accumulating losses and assembling a very specific kind of frustration — the kind that comes from scoring enough to win most weeks and losing anyway. The pitching is the diagnosis: 290 cumulative pitching points through three weeks was the warning, and the trend has not improved. Week 7 against Midland Red Weasel Dogs is a game the Spartans must win to avoid falling to 1-6 with the season’s first third already gone.

The Cascades Are Who They Say They Are
By Joe Falls  ·  The Longball Gazette

I covered the Detroit Tigers for thirty years. I watched Sparky Anderson manage games with the kind of quiet certainty that made everything look inevitable after the fact. You didn’t know it was coming. You just knew, watching him, that he knew something the other dugout didn’t. That is what the Troutdale Cascades look like right now. Five and one. Two five-hundred-point weeks. Two thousand eight hundred and thirty-five cumulative points. Three hundred and fourteen points clear of Goldy Goldy Gone, who is not a bad team. Not even a little bad. The Cascades are just better right now, and watching them operate gives you the feeling that they know it and nobody else does yet.

The number that matters this week is 504. Again. The Cascades have posted 504 points twice this season. The second time was this week, against Sidd Finch, who is a real team that had a terrible week. They won by 244 points. On a team where Jacob Misiorowski going 0.00 ERA for six innings is not the headline. That is depth. That is roster construction working at the system level, not the individual level.

Now let me talk about the BANK of Texas for a moment, because what they did this week deserves honest acknowledgment. One hundred and thirty-one pitching points from two starters. Cristopher Sanchez at 69.5 and Davis Martin at 61.5. Against Goldy Goldy Gone, who is the best-floor team in this league and still has the second-highest cumulative total. The BANK beat them. By 25 points. On the road. That is not a fluke. That is a team capable of beating anyone when the arms are all in on the same week.

The problem is the phrase “when.” When the BANK deploys correctly, they beat Goldy. When they don’t, they lose to the Sons of Pitches. Three and three is the exact record you’d expect from a team that alternates between excellent and incoherent. Week 7 against Tempe is the test. The Scorpions just posted 109 combined pitching points from two arms. If both teams bring their best rotation weeks simultaneously, this is the most interesting game on the board outside of Rats-Goldy.

Two more notes before I sit down. First: Tempe Scorpions, two and four, posted the week’s second and fourth-best pitching performances. Eduardo Rodriguez at a 0.59 ERA is not a fluke, it is a performance. They beat the Ithaca Spartans by 55 points and it was not that close. Second: the Ithaca Spartans have Bobby Witt Jr. on the roster. He scored 42 points. They are one and five. Those two sentences, sitting next to each other like that, are the most damning statistical indictment I can offer. Fix the supporting cast. Everything else takes care of itself.

What Consistency Costs
By Mitch Albom  ·  The Longball Gazette

There is a moment in competitive sport when a team stops being a contender and starts being something more specific: an inevitability. The Troutdale Cascades reached that moment somewhere around the third week of this season, and nothing in Week 6 suggested they have any interest in vacating it. Five wins and one loss is a record. Two thousand eight hundred and thirty-five points is a posture. It says: we are not winning close games. We are not grinding out margins. We are posting 504 points and letting the opponent figure out what to do about it.

I want to write about Andy Pages this week, because in a league full of big names and keeper investments, he is the Troutdale Gales player who led all hitters with 48 points and did it while playing on a team that is three and three. Pages at 48 points is not the story of the Gales’ week. He is the headline on a story that does not yet have a satisfying conclusion. The Gales have the league’s most explosive offense. They are also the team whose pitching staff has the lowest cumulative innings total in the league. That imbalance — offense elite, pitching thin — is not a new observation. It is now a documented pattern across six scoring periods. Something has to change or the pattern becomes the season.

The BANK of Texas fascinates me this week. Not just because they won, but because of what the win revealed. One hundred and thirty-one pitching points from two starters. A franchise that has spent six weeks looking for its identity produced its clearest self-portrait in a single week. The BANK, at their best, is a team with elite pitching and elite position players and the ability to beat anyone in this league. At their average, they are a team that loses to teams they should beat. The distance between those two versions of the BANK is the most interesting gap in MLBLONGBALL. Whether they can narrow it in Week 7 against a Tempe team that just showed their own pitching ceiling will tell us more about this franchise than the first six weeks combined.

Brooks Lee’s 37-point week for the Desert Rats and his ten-percent ownership surge tell a specific story about how this commissioner operates. Most teams in this league identify wire targets by reputation. The Rats identify them by output. Lee at rank 108 and trending sharply upward is the wire working the way it’s supposed to — rewarding the manager who looked past the name and focused on the number.

I keep coming back to the Sons of Pitches. One hundred and eighty-eight points. The lowest team score of the season. In a week where multiple starters on other rosters produced 40-plus points individually, the Sons’ total suggests a roster that is not failing occasionally — it is failing structurally. At two and four with the second-lowest cumulative total, the season is not technically over. It feels like a team that has given itself permission to be disappointed and is living comfortably inside that permission. The wire is open. It is not that hard to make a move. Sometimes the hardest thing in competition is admitting that what you have is not enough and doing something about it before it becomes too late to do anything at all.

The Ithaca Spartans have one win and five losses. Bobby Witt Jr. gave them 42 points and they lost. Aaron Judge is on this roster and this team has not found a way to make the players around him contribute at the level required. In the end, that is the saddest story in six weeks of MLBLONGBALL — not because the talent is absent, but because the talent is present and the opportunity is being wasted.
