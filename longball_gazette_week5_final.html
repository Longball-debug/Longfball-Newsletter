Week 5 Final — April 27–May 3, 2026
THE RATS ROAR BACK; SCORPIONS STING THE INDIANS; SIDD FINCH KEEPS ROLLING
Week 5 answered the league's biggest open question: are the Desert Rats still alive? The answer was 411 points. Meanwhile the Scorpions picked up their first win, Triple G posted 459 in a statement performance, and Sidd Finch, as always, simply won.
By the Longball Gazette Wire Staff
League Headquarters — Sunday, May 3, 2026

Four weeks of declining numbers. Four weeks of warning signs, quiet lineups, and the growing suspicion that something was fundamentally wrong in the desert. Then Week 5 happened. The Desert Rats posted 411 points against the Sons of Pitches, winning by 128.5 in the most convincing performance of their season. Max Meyer cracked the top-10 pitcher leaderboard at 33.5 points. The lineup finally produced across the board. Whatever had been ailing this roster — and the numbers suggested plenty — was not visible on Sunday night's final scorecard.

The Rats needed this. They needed it badly. After a four-week point trend of 528, 452, 325, 310, a bounce to 411 doesn't erase the structural questions, but it provides the one thing no analyst can manufacture: evidence. Week 5 is evidence. The question for Week 6 is whether it repeats.

The other story of the week belongs to the Tempe Scorpions, who walked into Newberry and left with a 397.5–310 win — 87.5 points, no asterisk, no controversy. Jordan Walker delivered 55.5 points in his first signature week as a Scorpion. Gavin Williams added 36.33 on the mound. The Scorpions have now demonstrated they can win a matchup cleanly when their roster fires.

Sidd Finch: Still Cruising, Still the Standard

Sidd Finch posted 464.33 — the week's highest team total — and dispatched the Troutdale Gales 464.33–337.17. Jesus Luzardo (61.67 pts) and Clay Holmes (55.83 pts) were the week's top two pitchers. Alec Burleson added 56 points at the plate. This is a roster that does not have bad weeks, and that is a more alarming characteristic at Week 5 than it was at Week 1. The Commish's note: "Sidd continues to cruise." The rest of the league has no more comfortable language for it either.

Triple G's Four-Pitcher Week Is a Warning Shot

Goldy Goldy Gone posted 459.17 against the Houston Gamblers and did it by placing four pitchers in the weekly top ten. Ranger Suarez (50.5 pts), Kris Bubic (41.5), Max Fried (36.67), and Emerson Hancock (35) all delivered in the same scoring period. That kind of rotation depth doesn't happen by accident. The Cascades continued their autopilot winning streak with 425.33 over the Weasel Dogs, powered by Kazuma Okamoto's 46.5-point debut week after his waiver claim.

The Scorpion in the Room
By Joe Falls  ·  The Longball Gazette

I have been doing this a long time. Long enough to know that trades, in fantasy baseball as in real baseball, are rarely as good or as bad as they look in the moment. The Desert Rats moved Jordan Walker to the Tempe Scorpions. At the time it was a transaction. A piece of business. A roster decision made with the information available. These things happen.

Then Week 5 happened. Jordan Walker posted 55.5 points for the Tempe Scorpions. He was the second-best hitter in the entire league. He was more than 20 points better than any hitter the Desert Rats put on the field. The Rats won their game — won it convincingly, by 128 points, which is the most convincing performance of their season. And yet the number I keep coming back to is 55.5. Because the Desert Rats could have had that number. They chose a different number instead.

I am not here to second-guess the commissioner. He is smarter than I am and he sees the roster in ways I don't. There may be factors in that trade that justify the outcome entirely. Maybe the piece he received back is going to produce a five-week run that makes this conversation look foolish. These things happen too. I have seen it many times.

What I know is this: the Rats are 4-1. Walker is a Scorpion. They both won this week. And somewhere in the desert, there is a number — 55.5 — that is going to follow this front office for the rest of the season whether it deserves to or not. That is the nature of trades. On Cruising
By Mitch Albom  ·  The Longball Gazette

The Commissioner's note on Sidd Finch this week was three words: "Sidd continues to cruise." I have been thinking about those three words since Sunday night, about what they contain and what they leave out.

Cruising is a word we use when something seems effortless. When the result is not in doubt. When the machinery is operating exactly as designed and the outcome is a formality rather than a contest. The Troutdale Gales scored 337 points this week. That is not a bad score. In four of the six matchups this week, 337 wins. Against Sidd Finch, 337 loses by 127. That gap — 127 points — is what cruising looks like from the outside.

What makes Sidd Finch interesting is not that they are good. There are other good teams in this league. What makes them interesting is that they are consistent. They do not have the weeks where the pitching disappears or the lineup goes cold or the schedule delivers a nightmare matchup at the wrong moment. They have Luzardo throwing 61 points. They have Holmes throwing 55. They have Burleson hitting 56. They have 290 more points underneath those three. That is not a hot week. That is a constructed roster performing to specification.

Week 6 brings the Troutdale Cascades to their house. The Cascades are 5-0 and have never lost. That is the one thing that might interrupt the cruise. I am not certain it will. I am not certain anything will. That is the most alarming sentence I have written about this league all season.
