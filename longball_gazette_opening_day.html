18 Years of Longball Begin Again Today
After the most competitive Free Agent Frenzy in league history, twelve teams prepare to settle the score the only way that matters — on the field. Week 1 runs March 25–29.
By Wire Staff, Longball Gazette
Mesa, AZ — Wednesday, March 25, 2026

The wait is over. After weeks of keeper announcements, a February Frenzy that produced historic bargains, and a draft that reshuffled the deck one final time, the 2026 MLBLONGBALL season tips off today with six head-to-head matchups that will set the tone for the next six months of fantasy combat.

The Desert Rats enter as the overwhelming pre-season favorite, fielding what may be the most formidable keeper tandem in league history: Shohei Ohtani — the only legitimate threat to score 1,200+ batting points alone — paired with Kyle Tucker at 4.78 fantasy points per game. Commish's squad also boasts Freddie Freeman, Bryce Harper, and Logan Gilbert anchoring what is a roster without a visible weak spot.

But a desert wind doesn't win a pennant. Eight other teams are punching their tickets to relevance on opening day. The Houston Gamblers are the most dangerous dark horse, concealing Jose Ramirez and Chris Sale behind a roster that looks mid-tier on paper but plays championship-level in the box score. The Newberry Indians — ever-shrewd, ever-patient — are quietly terrifying with Yamamoto, Lindor, and a batting order anchored by Gunnar Henderson and Ketel Marte.

Week 1 serves its traditional purpose: establishing narratives. Somebody's streaky power bat will go 0-for-the-week. Somebody's ace will give up six earned runs in his first start. Somebody's closer will blow a save, and the owners of that franchise will immediately threaten to drop him. This is the poetry of fantasy baseball, and MLBLONGBALL has been writing it for 18 seasons.

"You can't hide from the scoreboard. You either produce or you don't. Week 1 finds out who prepared and who panicked."

The Goldy Goldy Gone squad arrives as the most intriguing offensive unit in the league. Skubal (21.77 pts/game as SP) atop the pitching staff gives them an almost unfair advantage every fifth day, while Corbin Carroll (4.5 FP/G), Manny Machado, Christian Yelich, and Nico Hoerner provide lineup depth that holds up even on off days. They open against the Tempe Scorpions — a dangerous opponent featuring Vladdy Guerrero and Mookie Betts — in what may be the sleeper matchup of the opening week.

The Sidd Finch organization arrives at Opening Day with the league's most talked-about wildcard: Nolan McLean. The right-hander projects for 12.5 points per game — adequate, but the question is whether "adequate" is enough alongside the rest of a rotation that leans heavily on Jacob deGrom (health permitting) and Framber Valdez. If McLean delivers, Sidd is a playoff team. If he struggles, they may be chasing from day one.

◆ Matchup 1 — The Marquee  |  Home: Desert Rats  |  Away: Ithaca Spartans
Ithaca Spartans
Ithaca, MI  ·  Away

vs.
Desert Rats
Mesa, AZ  ·  Home
KEY: Ohtani (8.33 FP/G), Tucker (4.78 FP/G), Gilbert SP
Preview

The Gazette's Game of the Week. This is the only matchup featuring two teams from the top tier of every preseason power ranking, and the results will tell us whether Ithaca's Judge-Crochet tandem can generate enough points early to stay with a Rats roster that doesn't have a single below-average lineup spot.

Aaron Judge at 5.25 FP/G is legitimately the only individual bat that threatens Ohtani's output, and when Crochet takes the mound — projecting 19.88 points per start — the Spartans have a genuine shot at winning the pitching battle outright. Ithaca's lineup depth is thinner than the Rats, however: Adley Rutschman at catcher is fine, Bo Bichette and Steven Kwan provide contact, but this roster doesn't have the three-through-nine consistency that Mesa brings.

The X-factor is Gerrit Cole. At 18.14 FP/G in a reduced 2025 sample, a healthy Cole in Week 1 could swing the pitching ledger back toward Ithaca. But his history of early-season stumbles is well-documented.

The Pick: Desert Rats win, but Spartans keep it close

This matchup features two of the better-constructed rosters in the middle tier — and a fascinating pitching subplot. The BANK counters with Cristopher Sanchez at an eye-popping 18.22 FP/G; the Cascades answer with Chase Burns (17.98). The ace matchup alone could be the difference in a tight week.

At the plate, the BANK's big-two of Soto and Acuña project to outscore anything Troutdale can put forward, but the Cascades' depth — J-Rod, Raleigh, Kurtz, and a revived Maikel Garcia — gives them a legitimate chance to win the volume battle. The BANK's lineup has a notable vulnerability at catcher (Carson Kelly, just 2.83 FP/G) and their reserve pitching is soft.

Watch for the Cascades' Chandler Simpson (4.28 FP/G) — pure stolen base value in a points system that rewards SBs at 3 points each. He's a secret weapon.

The Pick: BANK of Texas, but Cascades steal a start

The Gazette's most unpredictable opener. McLean watch officially begins here. His Week 1 start is circled on every league calendar. A strong outing and Sidd becomes a genuine threat. A disaster and the narrative writes itself.

The Indians vs. the Gales is the matchup where the Newberry reputation meets its first real test of 2026. The Indians are patient, calculated drafters who arrive with Yamamoto (18.08 FP/G projected) as their trump card and an extraordinarily balanced lineup that goes eight-deep without a hole. Henderson at 4.11 FP/G at shortstop, Marte at 4.55 at 2B — this team simply doesn't give you easy outs.

The Gales counter with genuine firepower: Elly De La Cruz is one of the five most explosive fantasy bats in the league when he's locked in. Schwarber (3.86 FP/G) and Brent Rooker (3.79) give them a power-heavy middle of the order, and Jesus Luzardo (15.47/G) is a legitimate top-15 fantasy starter.

The Indians' edge in pitching depth — they carry Yamamoto, Bubic, and Blake Snell on reserve — feels decisive here unless De La Cruz goes on a tear.

The Pick: Newberry Indians — staff depth decides it

Do not sleep on this one. This matchup features two of the best pitchers in the league — Paul Skenes (20.81 FP/G) for the Weasels versus Chris Sale (19.33 FP/G) for the Gamblers — and two rosters that have been criminally underestimated in preseason coverage, including by this publication.

The Weasels' Caminero (3.86 FP/G) and Jazz Chisholm Jr. (3.86) give them two legitimate contributors, and Skenes' projection makes them formidable every fifth day. But their lineup behind Trea Turner and Caminero gets thin faster than the Gamblers'.

Column — The Sporting News
A Baseball Town Needs Baseball
By Joe Falls · The Detroit News · J.G. Taylor Spink Award, 2001

Opening Day. Two words that never get old.

I've covered a lot of opening days. Real ones, at Tiger Stadium, where the grass was so green it hurt your eyes and everybody in the ballpark believed this was finally the year. And now I cover this one — MLBLONGBALL's eighteenth — and I'll tell you something: the feeling isn't all that different.

Every manager in this league walked into today thinking they had the team. The Desert Rats think Ohtani covers all sins. The Gamblers think Ramirez and Sale make them dangerous enough to run with anybody. Sidd Finch thinks McLean is going to be something special. They're all right, and they're all wrong, and that's what makes Opening Day beautiful.

Let me tell you what I saw when I went through these rosters. I saw teams that drafted for the scoreboard and teams that drafted for their hearts. The Cascades drafted Mariners again. I admire the loyalty and I question the strategy, but hey — loyalty's not the worst thing a man can have.

I saw the BANK of Texas pay one dollar for Juan Soto and one dollar for Ronald Acuña. One dollar. I was at the Vickrey auction when that happened and the silence in the room lasted about four seconds before somebody said something that can't be printed in a family newspaper.

The team I keep coming back to is the Gamblers. Nobody's picking them to win this thing. That's fine. The Gamblers have Jose Ramirez, who hits the ball like it owes him money, and Chris Sale, who at 36 years old still makes hitters look foolish. You don't need to be flashy to be dangerous.

The Rats are the story, I know that. Ohtani at 8.33 fantasy points per game is a number I've never seen in 18 years of covering this league. Eighteen years. The man is legitimately his own weather system. You don't beat the Desert Rats — you hope Ohtani has a slow week, and even then you're probably losing.

But that's what they said about every great team before the season started. And then May happens. And then July happens. And then somebody's closer blows three saves in a row and suddenly the whole thing looks different.

Play ball, gentlemen. Eighteen years, and I still get butterflies.

That's all I've got. That's enough.

Column — Detroit Free Press
The Season They'll Remember
By Mitch Albom · Detroit Free Press

His name was Nolan McLean, and on a Tuesday night in February, his name was written on a keeper form and deposited into a sealed envelope by a man named Sidd Finch, who believed in him.

That's what a fantasy season really is, when you strip away the spreadsheets and the auction prices and the projected points per game. It's an act of faith. You believe in a player. You commit to him on paper. And then you wait to see if the universe agrees.

Nolan McLean is 24 years old. He pitched in 26 games last season and finished with a 3.92 ERA and 137 strikeouts across 147 innings. Those are good numbers. They are not great numbers. They are the numbers of a young man still learning who he is on a baseball field — and that is exactly the kind of player that fantasy baseball was built to celebrate.

The Desert Rats don't need faith. They have Shohei Ohtani, which is as close to certainty as this game allows. The Spartans have Aaron Judge and Garrett Crochet, names that belong on marquees. The Gamblers have Jose Ramirez, who needs no introduction in any zip code.

But somewhere in Hainesville, Illinois, the manager of Sidd Finch — a man who has played in this league for eighteen seasons and knows exactly what he's doing — looked at a 24-year-old pitcher and saw something worth keeping.

I think about that a lot when I watch sports. The moment before it happens. The unsigned contract. The blank scorecard. The roster before the first pitch. Everything is still possible. No one has failed yet. No one has won yet. It's the purest moment in athletics.

The Newberry Indians have been in this league almost since the beginning. They draft quietly and win methodically. You won't find them in the flashy headlines on draft day — you'll find them in October, still playing when half the league has given up. That's not luck. That's a philosophy. It's also, if we're being honest, a little annoying for everyone else.

The Houston Gamblers remind me of every team I ever covered that was supposed to be good but not great. Jose Ramirez is the kind of player who makes other players better by proximity. He hits, he runs, he grinds. Chris Sale still throws the ball like a man half his age. Somewhere in a season-opening ceremony, I hope somebody says his name out loud and appreciates what that still means.

Opening Day is not about the standings. It never is. It's about the moment. It's about eighteen managers — and the people in their lives who have to hear about fantasy baseball over dinner — believing that this time, their team, their picks, their faith in a 24-year-old pitcher from the Mets' system, is going to be the thing that makes the difference.

Play ball. Everything is still possible.

— Albom

Opinion — Big Lou's Corner
Big Lou's Opening Day Verdicts
By Lou "Big Lou" Palmieri · Baseball Lifer · No Advanced Degree Required

Okay. I've gone through all twelve rosters. I've looked at every number. And I've got things to say, so everybody sit down.

The Desert Rats are LOADED. I said it before the Frenzy, I said it after the Frenzy, and I'm saying it again now that I've seen the full rosters. Ohtani at 8.33 points per game is not a number — it's a cheat code. Freeman and Harper in the same lineup? Those are two first-basemen who each put up over 550 fantasy points last year. You can't guard all of them.

But here's what gets me about the Gamblers and I don't think people are talking about this enough. Chris Sale went 19.33 fantasy points per START last year. Jose Ramirez is FIVE POINTS A GAME. Those two alone could carry a mediocre roster. And the Gamblers don't HAVE a mediocre roster — they got Olson, Pasquantino, Neto, Springer, and a deep bullpen. Somebody put a sleeper alert on this team!

Now let me talk about the Cascades, since apparently nobody's going to stop them from drafting half the Seattle roster. Julio Rodriguez — fine, great. Cal Raleigh — okay, legitimate. Chase Burns — very good. But Jacob Misiorowski? Bryan Woo? Chandler Simpson? You got enough of these Mariners players you should just rename the team the Fantasy Seattle Mariners and call it a day. I say this with affection. But still.

The Weasels are sneaky dangerous and I want to be on record. Paul Skenes is going to be the best pitcher nobody talks about because everyone's talking about Skubal and Crochet. But 20.81 points per start? That's within a rounding error of Skubal. And Caminero had 37 home runs last year at 22 YEARS OLD. The Weasels could finish third in this league and people would act surprised. Don't be surprised.

The Sons of Pitches have Yordan Alvarez, Fernando Tatis, and Roman Anthony in their outfield. Let me repeat that. YORDAN ALVAREZ. FERNANDO TATIS. ROMAN ANTHONY. How is this team ranked ninth in some projections I've seen? Has everybody lost their minds? Okay so the infield is a little thin. Okay so Arraez hits singles all day and doesn't give you power. I get it. But that outfield PRODUCES.

Sidd Finch and McLean — I'm not gonna lie to you. I go back and forth. The projection is 12.5 points per game, which is a perfectly cromulent number. But deGrom is 37 and every start is a gift. Valdez is 32 and been great. If McLean hits 15+ per game, this rotation is a Tier II rotation and Sidd is a playoff team. I believe in Bobby Witt Jr. enough to pick them to get there. But McLean has to deliver. He's the season in one pitcher.

Last thing. The BANK of Texas. I know they paid $1 for Soto and Acuña and I know that was brilliant. But I've watched this franchise for years. I know their patterns. If this team struggles by May, there will be roster moves. There will be closer acquisitions that make no sense. There will be panic trades. I'm not saying it's gonna happen. I'm saying I've seen this movie before and I know how it ends.

Have a great season, everyone. Except the teams playing the Gamblers. You're on your own.

— Big Lou, Baseball Lifer
