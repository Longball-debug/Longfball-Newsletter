Opening Week — Midpoint Report
FINCH FLIES; RATS SCRAMBLE; MIDLAND HITS THE FLOOR
The biggest shock of Opening Week isn't who's winning — it's who's winning it. Sidd Finch, last in preseason keeper rankings, leads the entire league through five days of baseball.
By the Longball Gazette Wire Staff
League Headquarters — Sunday, March 29, 2026

Nobody picked Sidd Finch. Not in the keeper rankings, not in the preseason projections, not in the commissioner's own private predictions. The team that entered 2026 with an unranked Nolan McLean occupying a roster spot reserved for top-two picks now sits atop the entire MLBLONGBALL league through the first five days of the season with a staggering 287.33 points — the highest total in the league and a lead that has left the field in collective disbelief.

Meanwhile, the team that was supposed to be running away with everything — the Desert Rats, armed with the Shohei Ohtani–Kyle Tucker keeper tandem that the preseason press called "the most dangerous duo in league history" — is sitting in fifth place, having executed more roster transactions in five days than some teams make in a month.

This is Week 1 of 2026. The projections have been shredded. The spring training theories have met live baseball. And the Longball Gazette is here to tell you exactly what happened.

Two Teams Cross 200 First

The race to 200 points is a reliable early barometer of roster strength, and this year it produced an unlikely pair of leaders. Goldy Goldy Gone cleared 200 first, riding Tarik Skubal's dominant start and a balanced attack anchored by catcher Shea Langeliers. But it was Sidd Finch who didn't just cross 200 — they blew past it and kept going, posting 287.33 while the rest of the league watched in stunned silence.

The Finch pitching staff has been the story of the week. Two strikeouts per inning pitched. Seventy strikeouts in thirty-five innings of work. In a scoring system that rewards Ks at 1.5 points each, that kind of dominance adds up with terrifying efficiency.

The Rats Are Restless

Commissioner's own Desert Rats have been the league's most active team in the transaction wire, logging over twenty moves since the draft — cycling through relievers, dropping household names, and reshuffling their lineup at a pace that suggests panic, experimentation, or both. The good news: they're still leading the Ithaca Spartans by 43 points in their Week 1 matchup. The bad news: their pitching staff has surrendered enough strikeouts to their batters to partially cancel out the offensive production. Joey Wiemer, not Ohtani or Tucker, is their top scoring hitter this week. That was not in the brochure.

Five Days In
By Joe Falls  ·  The Longball Gazette

Nobody told Sidd Finch they were supposed to lose. That's the thing about this league. The teams that were supposed to carry it — the Desert Rats with Ohtani and Tucker, the Ithaca Spartans with Judge and Crochet — they're not embarrassing themselves. But they're not running away with anything either.

Sidd Finch is at 287 points. Two strikeouts per inning pitched. If you watched this team during the Free Agent Frenzy, you thought it was over before it started. McLean unranked. Witt carrying the load alone. And now here they are, leading the league by twenty-seven points over Goldy Goldy Gone.

I've seen this before. Not in fantasy baseball — in real baseball. The team everyone wrote off finds something in the first week that nobody expected. Sometimes it holds. More often it doesn't. But for right now, Sidd Finch is playing the best baseball in this league and they deserve to be called for it.

What I want to know about the Desert Rats is this: twenty-plus transactions in five days. Twenty. That is a lot of decisions. Some of them have worked — Joey Wiemer is their best hitter this week and he was a waiver claim. But some of them haven't, and when you're constantly rearranging the furniture, you lose track of what the room was supposed to look like.

Ohtani is on that roster. Tucker is on that roster. One of them ought to be on a leaderboard somewhere. When they show up — and they will — the Rats are going to be very hard to beat. Until then, fifth place with the best keeper pair in league history is a story worth telling.

And Midland Red Weasel Dogs at ninety-one points. With a sub-zero day. In the first week of the season. I'll leave that one alone. Some numbers speak for themselves.

The Myth of the Perfect Roster
By Mitch Albom  ·  The Longball Gazette

There is a moment in every season — usually early, usually quiet — when the game stops being theoretical and becomes personal. This is that moment for the MLBLONGBALL league. Five days in. Real points on the board. Real decisions already made and already regretted.

Consider the Troutdale Gales. They hold the top two hitters in the entire league — Mike Trout leading the way, Brendan Donovan right behind him. They had a 67.5-point day. They are losing. How does that happen? It happens because this game, like all games, is not about any single thing. You cannot win on offense alone. You cannot build something real on one category of excellence while other parts of the roster quietly surrender.

There is a lesson in the Gales that applies beyond fantasy baseball. We often focus on what we have that others don't — the star, the advantage, the edge. We forget to look at what we are missing. The Gales have stars. They are missing something else.

And then there is Sidd Finch. Named, I am told, after a mythological pitcher. Playing, right now, like one. The beauty of sports — real or fantasy — is that the story does not always follow the expected arc. The unranked team at the top. The heavily favored team in fifth. These are not accidents. They are reminders that preparation matters, but execution matters more.

Every manager in this league has spent weeks building something on paper. Now the paper is gone and only the field remains. Some rosters are translating. Some are not. The ones that are tend to share a common quality: they were built not just with talent in mind, but with this specific scoring system in mind.

Strikeouts matter here. Wins matter. Blown saves hurt. Errors compound. The managers who understood that going in are at the top of this leaderboard. The ones who are learning it now are scrambling to catch up — twenty transactions deep, still searching for the right combination. The season is long. But five days is already long enough to know something true.

Big Lou's Corner: I Told You So (Almost)
By Lou Palmieri, Baseball Lifer  ·  The Longball Gazette

Alright. I was wrong about one thing. ONE thing. I said the Desert Rats were your 2026 champions before the season started. I stand by the logic. I do NOT stand by the fact that Joey Wiemer — a waiver wire pickup, a WAIVER WIRE PICKUP — is their best hitter through five games while Ohtani and Tucker are apparently taking a spa week somewhere in Scottsdale.

But here's what kills me more than the Rats underperforming: SIDD FINCH IS WINNING. The team I wrote off. The team with the unranked keeper. The team that came into this season with a Ferrari engine and a station wagon attached to it. Two hundred and eighty-seven points. TWO HUNDRED AND EIGHTY-SEVEN. That's not a pace. That's an accusation.

You know what that pitching staff is doing? Two strikeouts an inning. TWO. In five-point scoring for wins and saves and 1.5 per strikeout, that math adds up to a mugging. The Sons of Pitches didn't lose a game. They lost a debate about whether baseball is fair.

Meanwhile, Goldy Goldy Gone is over there averaging twenty-one points a day like they've got a spreadsheet running on autopilot. Skubal is pitching like the ace everybody said he was. Langeliers is hitting like he belongs in the top five. I'm not saying Goldy wins this league. I'm saying right now, today, they look like a real team.

Midland Red Weasel Dogs. NINETY-ONE POINTS. And they had a day — AN ENTIRE DAY — where they went minus. MINUS! In a fantasy baseball season! That means their players were so bad their earned runs and strikeouts and double plays cost them more points than their hits and wins produced. That's not a slump. That's a lifestyle.

The Cascades are twelve points back against the BANK and they're coming. The Newberry Indians are beating a team with the top two hitters in the league on pure pitching guts. The Houston Gamblers are quietly sitting at eighth in the power rankings and leading their game by seventy-five. This league is a mess and I love every single minute of it. Seven days to go. Don't touch that dial.
