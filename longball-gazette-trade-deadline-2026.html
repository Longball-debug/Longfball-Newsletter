Fireworks Early: Every Roster's Cracks, Exposed Before Midnight on the Fourth

By Joe Falls, Longball Gazette Staff

With the Trade Deadline closing at midnight on the Fourth of July, there's no more time for general managers around MLBLONGBALL to admire their rosters from a distance. We pulled the full league ledger — every starter, every reserve, every man on the injury wire — and ran it slot by slot against the league averages to find exactly where each of these twelve outfits is bleeding production. What follows is not a hunch. It's a diagnosis.

A note on method: this report is built entirely from current roster construction and cumulative fantasy production (FPts) at each lineup slot, cross-checked against league-wide averages per position. It does not reflect the actual win-loss standings, which the Commissioner's office tracks separately. Think of it as a scouting report on the chassis, not the scoreboard — though the two tend to rhyme.

Deadline Reminders: Vickrey (FAAB) bidding remains in effect through the break, with the waiver wire processing Sundays at 7:00 PM MST. The 12-starts-per-period cap is still very much alive for any club stockpiling arms for a stretch run. Trades must be completed by midnight, July 4th.

Desert Rats RATS

The Read

The Rats sit atop the entire league in roster production, and it isn't close — Shohei Ohtani (357.0), Bryce Harper (351.0) and Freddie Freeman (314.5) anchor a lineup with thump at every corner, while a stacked rotation (Logan Gilbert, Logan Webb, Dylan Cease, Nathan Eovaldi, Seth Lugo) gives this club more usable starts than it can even play. This is a club built to win now.

The soft spot is the middle infield slot, where Corey Seager has produced just 129.0 points — more than 100 below the league-average MI bat — and a bullpen (Barnett, Kerkering, Melton, Weaver) that's serviceable but unspectacular. Zack Gelof (2B), Trent Grisham (OF) and Logan Henderson (SP) are all currently on the injury wire.

Deadline Move

With three healthy, productive starters stashed on the bench (Cease, Eovaldi, Lugo all rostered behind the active five), the Rats have the rarest asset in the league: surplus front-line pitching. Flip one for a bat at MI and a save-chaser for the pen, and this is the team to beat in the second half.

The Read

No staff in the league produces like Troutdale's. Jacob Misiorowski (419.0) leads every pitcher in MLBLONGBALL, and he's got Chase Burns (313.8) and Braxton Ashcraft (294.3) for company — plus David Bednar, Mason Miller and Cade Smith stabilizing the bullpen. This is, by the numbers, the best pitching staff in the Gazette's coverage area.

The offense is the drag. Catcher is a genuine void — Cal Raleigh, normally an MVP-caliber bat, is producing just 99.5 points, the worst output at the position leaguewide. CI (Bryce Eldridge, 135.0) is similarly thin. Tyler Soderstrom (OF) and Blake Snell (SP) are both shelved.

Deadline Move

Cascades have five rostered starters and could deal from a position of absurd strength. A change-of-scenery package — spare SP for a bat at catcher or corner infield — fixes the only real hole on a roster that's otherwise championship-built.

The Gales own the league's most productive offense, full stop — Andy Pages (338.0), Kyle Schwarber (310.5) and Pete Alonso (310.0) lead an outfield-and-corners attack with no real holes, and Nico Hoerner anchors second base at an excellent 305.0.

This is the textbook buyer: elite, deep offense, thin rotation. A change-the-deadline-day trade — spare bat for a controllable starter — turns the Gales from a scary offense into a complete club.
