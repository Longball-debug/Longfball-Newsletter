THREE KINGS STAND ALONE; BANK SITS ON ITS HANDS; SONS SHOCK THE HOUSE
Goldy, the Rats, and Newberry Indians are 2-0 and pulling away. The BANK of Texas is 0-2 with 188 at-bats — a number that deserves its own exhibit at the Hall of Shame. And the Sons of Pitches, everybody's punching bag, just beat them by 159 points.
By the Longball Gazette Wire Staff
League Headquarters — Sunday, April 12, 2026

Two weeks into the 2026 MLBLONGBALL season, the picture at the top is becoming uncomfortably clear. Goldy Goldy Gone has scored 1,181.5 points. The Newberry Indians have scored 1,030. The Desert Rats have scored 980.5. All three are 2-0. Everyone else is watching from a growing distance and wondering when exactly this happened.

The week's most complete performance belonged to the Desert Rats, who posted 452.33 points and outscored the Tempe Scorpions by 122 points while simultaneously making what appears to be a dozen roster transactions before breakfast each morning. Bryce Harper (43.5 pts) finally gave the commissioner something to talk about besides Joey Wiemer. Logan Gilbert's 41.5-point pitching performance provided the rotation backbone the Rats had been scrambling to find since Opening Day. The transaction log from Week 2 alone reads like a man frantically rearranging deck chairs, but the ship is sailing and that is the only thing that matters.

The Sons of Pitches delivered the upset of the week — and arguably the upset of the young season. Down 0-1, labeled a basement team, facing a BANK of Texas squad holding Juan Soto and Ronald Acuña Jr., the Sons went out and posted 410 points to the BANK's 251. The BANK's problem was not their stars. Their problem was 188 at-bats — the lowest total in the league by 44 plate appearances. Players on that roster sat on benches all week while the lineup card had empty slots that Soto and Acuña could not fill alone. This is not bad luck. This is mismanagement.

The Innings Theorem Holds

For the second consecutive week, every team that threw more innings won their matchup. Goldy's 80 innings of pitching at a sterling WHIP carried them past Midland's power surge. Newberry's 72 disciplined innings beat back a Cascades team that couldn't find a closer. The Troutdale Gales led the league with 22 stolen bases and lost anyway because 51.1 innings pitched is not a pitching staff — it is a plea for help. The theorem is simple: in this scoring system, volume pitching wins weeks. The teams ranked 1 through 3 have all figured this out. Most of the rest are still learning it the hard way.

The BANK Needs an Auditor

One hundred and eighty-eight at-bats. In a full week of baseball. The league average was 244. The BANK of Texas left at least 56 plate appearances on the table — which, at even a modest points-per-AB rate, represents 30 to 40 points of pure waste. Soto is on the roster. Acuña is on the roster. The BANK scored 251 points total. Something in that lineup is dead weight and it is costing them games that their star players are not being given the opportunity to win. This is the definition of a solvable problem, which makes it all the more frustrating that it remains unsolved entering Week 3.

Two Weeks In, The Picture Is Clear
By Joe Falls  ·  The Longball Gazette

You want to know what this league looks like after two weeks? I'll tell you. There are three teams playing a different game than everyone else, and nine teams watching them do it. Goldy Goldy Gone is 2-0 with 1,181 points scored. The Desert Rats are 2-0 with 980. The Newberry Indians are 2-0 with 1,030. Three undefeated teams. The rest of the field has a combined 11-13 record. That is not competitive balance. That is a hierarchy forming in real time.

I want to talk about the BANK of Texas because somebody has to. One hundred and eighty-eight at-bats. In seven days of baseball. The Sons of Pitches — the team they were supposed to beat comfortably — had 232 at-bats and scored 159 more points. Juan Soto and Ronald Acuña Jr. are on that roster. They are two of the best hitters in the sport. And the bench around them is idle, dormant, and costing the BANK games that the stars don't deserve to lose. This is a solvable problem. The fact that it has not been solved in two weeks is the story of this team's season so far.

The Midland Red Weasel Dogs deserve more credit than they're getting. Thirty-nine home runs. James Wood at 67 points. They took Goldy Goldy Gone to the wire in the closest game of the week. They are a genuinely dangerous team that the rest of the league is underestimating because they lost to the Gamblers in Week 1.

The Desert Rats won again. They are 2-0. They made enough transactions this week to staff a mid-market front office. Bryce Harper finally showed up, which is good, because the theory of a team built around Ohtani and Tucker requires that Bryce Harper also be Bryce Harper. Logan Gilbert was excellent. The Rats are winning and the methodology is still a mystery — but the results are not, and in fantasy baseball, that is all that matters.

One more thing about Ithaca. Eighty-five strikeouts allowed to their own hitters. That is eighty-five points surrendered before a single earned run was calculated. Aaron Judge is batting .235 or thereabouts on that roster. Garrett Crochet is doing his job. The players around them are not. If I were managing Ithaca, I would look at that 85-K number and make four to six moves on Monday morning before coffee. That number is a diagnosis, not a coincidence.

Three weeks from now, we'll know if the top three can hold. My hunch is two of them can.

The Numbers We Choose Not to See
By Mitch Albom  ·  The Longball Gazette

There is a number sitting in the middle of this week's statistics that deserves more attention than it has received. One hundred and eighty-eight. That is how many at-bats the BANK of Texas generated this week. Not one hundred and eighty-eight runs. Not one hundred and eighty-eight hits. One hundred and eighty-eight opportunities to put the ball in play — and the league average was two hundred and forty-four.

In the language of this scoring system, an at-bat is not just a moment in a game. It is a chance. A chance for a home run worth four points. A chance for a stolen base worth three. A chance to drive in runs, reach base, contribute to a win. When you leave those chances sitting on a bench — when your roster has players who are fully healthy, fully eligible, and fully inactive — you are not managing a fantasy team. You are holding assets that are accruing no value while the rest of the market moves without you.

The Troutdale Gales scored 22 stolen bases this week. They are the fastest team in this league. And yet they threw only 51.1 innings — the fewest in the entire league. Speed without pitching is like writing a great first act with no second act. The audience will applaud the opening. They will leave before the ending. The Gales are one bad pitching week from a loss that their lineup cannot absorb.

I think about Sidd Finch throwing 79 strikeouts and walking 44 batters and losing to the Houston Gamblers. There is something almost poetic about it — the team that was defined by pitching dominance in Week 1 meeting the limit of that dominance in Week 2. Forty-four walks is not an anomaly. It is a pattern. The teams that will beat Sidd Finch this season are the teams that put the ball in the air early and often. The Gamblers discovered this. The lesson will spread.

What strikes me most after two weeks is not the teams that are winning. It is the teams that are losing in ways they do not yet fully understand. Ithaca's 85 batting strikeouts. The BANK's 188 at-bats. The Gamblers' perpetual absence from the saves column. These are not random misfortunes. They are patterns, and patterns in fantasy baseball are the closest thing to fate. The managers who identify them now and act have a chance. The managers who wait will look at their records in June and wonder where May went.

The best teams win through systems. Goldy wins through innings. Newberry wins through depth. The Rats win through volume — in transactions, in decisions, in relentless adjustment. You can disagree with any methodology. You cannot argue with a 2-0 record.

Big Lou's Corner: I Have Some Notes
By Lou Palmieri, Baseball Lifer  ·  The Longball Gazette

Alright. Let's GO. The Desert Rats are 2-0 and I am taking full credit for predicting this because I said they were the 2026 champions in February and nothing that has happened since changes my position. The methodology — fifty-plus transactions, claiming Willy Adames and dropping him and winning him back in a Vickrey auction like some kind of fantasy baseball hostage negotiation — is completely INSANE. But they are WINNING. I cannot argue with 2-0. I will not argue with 2-0. When Bryce Harper posts 43.5 points and Logan Gilbert gives you 41.5, you are playing championship-caliber roster baseball even if it looks like you're assembling it blindfolded from a spinning wheel.

NOW. The BANK of Texas. I need everyone to sit down for this. One hundred and eighty-eight at-bats. I have been doing this a long time. I have seen mismanaged rosters. I have seen bad drafts. I have seen teams lose because their closer blew three saves in a week. I have NEVER seen a team with Juan Soto and Ronald Acuña Jr. on the roster generate one hundred and eighty-eight at-bats in a seven-day period. That's not underperforming. That's BENCHING your best players by accident. The Sons of Pitches beat them by a hundred and fifty-nine points. THE SONS OF PITCHES. The team that lost to Sidd Finch by two hundred and eight points in Week 1!

Goldy Goldy Gone is my new pick for the league. I know I said the Rats. I stand by the Rats. But Goldy has THREE players in the top-ten hitters list AND the pitching staff AND eighty innings pitched. That is not a team, that's a heist in progress.

The Newberry Indians have four pitchers in the top ten. FOUR. Bradley, Bubic, Rasmussen, AND Springs. That's not a rotation. That's an assembly line. They are 2-0 and they play the 0-2 BANK of Texas next week and I am genuinely concerned for the BANK's morale. Actually, I'm not. Fix your roster. Use your bench. Claim a closer. These are not advanced strategies. These are basic obligations.

Ithaca Spartans: eighty-five batting strikeouts. I'm going to say it slowly. Eighty. Five. Each one costs you a point. That is negative eighty-five points from one statistical category. Aaron Judge is on your roster and he's been batting .235. Garrett Crochet is DOING HIS JOB and the team is 0-2. Someone around those two is eating the season alive and the manager needs to find that someone, cut them, and replace them with Daulton Varsho who has been sitting on the free agent wire all week at thirty-nine and a half points. UNCLAIMED. In a twelve-team league. With a team that needs contact hitters. The Varsho situation is keeping me up at night.

James Wood hit sixty-seven points for Midland. The Houston Gamblers hit forty-two home runs AND STILL HAVE ZERO SAVES. The Troutdale Gales have twenty-two stolen bases and fifty-one innings pitched which is like showing up to a marathon with sprinting shoes and no water. I love this league and it is making me absolutely crazy. Week 3. Don't miss it.
