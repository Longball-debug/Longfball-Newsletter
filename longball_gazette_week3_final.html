THREE STILL STANDING; CASCADES DETONATE; BANK WAKES UP AND STILL LOSES
Goldy, the Rats, and Newberry all survive — but Newberry takes their first L after the BANK posts 473 points, three top-six pitchers, and still falls short. The Cascades post the 2026 season high. Sidd Finch hits a new low with 4 home runs. And Jeremiah Jackson outscores Ohtani and Tucker on the same roster.
By the Longball Gazette Wire Staff
League Headquarters — Sunday, April 20, 2026

The verified numbers from Scoring Period 3 tell a story of extremes. The Troutdale Cascades posted 500.5 points — the 2026 season high — generating 126 strikeouts in 166 innings with only 15 losses allowed, the fewest of any top team. At the opposite end: Sidd Finch managed 273 points on 4 home runs and a league-high 78 singles. In a format that pays 4 points per home run and 1 point per single, those two facts are a complete diagnosis of what went wrong.

The Newberry Indians suffered their first loss and the numbers explain exactly why. The Indians led the entire league in innings pitched (184.667), strikeouts (129), and saves-holds (50). They lost by 50 points. The BANK of Texas had Cristopher Sanchez (43 pts, 1.50 ERA), Michael King (43 pts, 0.82 ERA), and Bailey Ober (41.67 pts) — three pitchers in the top six league-wide — all in the same week. Both teams played well. The BANK played better. Newberry drops to 2-1. The BANK records their first win. The standings reflect exactly what happened on the field.

The Desert Rats are 3-0. Weekly outputs: 528.167, 452.333, 325.833. The decline is 40+ points per week and accelerating. Jeremiah Jackson (seasonal rank #118, 72% ownership) produced 47 points and outscored both blockbuster keepers on his own team. The good news is they won. The bad news is the Cascades just posted 500 points and the schedule gets harder from here.

The Cascades Are the League's Most Dangerous 2-1 Team

Five hundred points. Balanced at 710 hitting / 741 pitching — the most even construction of any top-three team. George Kirby (41.67 pts, 2.02 ERA) and Mason Miller (39 pts, 0.00 ERA) anchored a rotation that held losses to just 15. Only 137.2 points behind Goldy in cumulative standings. Their one persistent gap — the saves vacancy — showed improvement (50 SVH this week). One dedicated closer makes this roster the most frightening team not named Goldy Goldy Gone.

Sidd Finch: Four Home Runs Is Not a Slump

Four home runs in a league where HR=4 pts. Seventy-eight singles in a league where 1B=1 pt. The three-week scoring decline (676→320→273) is the steepest in MLBLONGBALL. Framber Valdez (41.5 pts, 1.38 ERA, 2 QS) was excellent. The offense is the problem. At 1-2 the identity of this roster needs to change before Week 4 becomes a turning point toward irrelevance.

The Data Is Clear. Now What?
By Joe Falls  ·  The Longball Gazette

After three weeks I can tell you this with confidence: Goldy Goldy Gone is the best team in this league because they have no exploitable weakness. Four-point-nine-two points per game — the league’s highest. Nico Hoerner went 9-for-26 for 49.5 points. Munetaka Murakami at 6.75 per game. Mick Abel at 0.00 ERA as a depth starter. This is what roster depth looks like. Not a star and eleven prayer requests. A machine with interchangeable parts that all work. Three wins without a peak performance in the last two weeks. That is what I mean when I say Goldy is the standard.

I want to talk about how the Newberry Indians lost because the manner of it matters. They led the entire league in innings pitched (184.667), strikeouts (129), and saves-holds (50) this week. They lost by fifty points to the BANK of Texas. Not because they played poorly — because Cristopher Sanchez (43 pts, 1.50 ERA), Michael King (43 pts, 0.82 ERA), and Bailey Ober (41.67 pts) all pitched in the same week and all landed in the top six pitchers in the league, combining for 127.67 points. That is not bad luck for Newberry. That is the BANK finally playing the game they were supposed to be playing since February. One of these weeks was going to happen. It happened in Week 3.

The Desert Rats are 3-0. Their weekly point totals: 528, 452, 325. That line has one direction. Jeremiah Jackson — rank 118, ownership 72% entering the week — outscored Ohtani and Tucker on the same roster. A waiver claim outperforming two blockbuster keepers is a story, not a strategy. The Rats are winning on favorable scheduling more than dominant execution. The matchup against Sidd Finch in Week 4 should be manageable. But the schedule hardens and the declining output cannot continue indefinitely without consequences.

One thing about Sidd Finch that the numbers make undeniable. Four home runs. In a league where home runs are worth four points each. While leading the league with seventy-eight singles at one point apiece. This is not a slump. It is an identity. Framber Valdez gave them 41.5 points and a 1.38 ERA — a legitimate performance. Everyone else produced 231.5 points on the back of singles and hope. At 1-2 with the steepest scoring decline in the league, the Finch offense needs to be rebuilt around power before Week 4 becomes a turning point toward irrelevance.

Will Warren of the Midland Red Weasel Dogs: 46.33 points, 17 strikeouts in 10.2 innings, 1.69 ERA, 14.34 K/9. That is the most dominant per-inning strikeout performance of the week. He and Paul Skenes (40 pts, 0.90 ERA) were two of the top eight pitchers in the league. Their team scored 282 points. The hitting (185 pts, 11th) is what separates the Dogs from contention. Warren and Skenes deserve better from the lineup behind them.

And finally: the Tempe Scorpions are 0-3 with 1,271 cumulative points. Gavin Williams (46 pts, 2.25 ERA) was the week’s second-best pitcher in a loss. They have outscored seven other teams in cumulative totals and have zero wins. I covered Detroit sports for thirty years. I have seen bad luck. This qualifies. The wins are in there. The schedule needs to stop eating them alive.

The Architecture of What Works
By Mitch Albom  ·  The Longball Gazette

There is a number in this week’s data that deserves more attention than a percentage sign. Seventy-eight. That is how many singles Sidd Finch recorded this week. It is the league’s highest total. Against it: four home runs, the league’s lowest. In a scoring system that pays four points for the latter and one for the former, this is not a neutral trade. It is a systematic underperformance of the available reward structure. Framber Valdez (41.5 pts, 1.38 ERA) is performing at an elite level and has been paired with an offense that is philosophically opposed to the format it plays in. That is not Valdez’s problem. It is a construction problem that 78 singles in a week make impossible to ignore.

The Troutdale Cascades’ 500.5-point week is the most instructive performance in three weeks of this season. Not because of the number alone — though it is extraordinary — but because of how it was constructed. Two hundred and seventeen hitting points. Two hundred and eighty-three pitching points. The balance between those two numbers (710 cumulative hitting / 741 cumulative pitching) is the most even of any top-three team in the league. They are not winning on a dominant category. They are winning on the absence of a weak one. That is a different kind of excellence and it is worth naming precisely.

The Newberry Indians lost for the first time and I find myself more interested in how they lost than in the loss itself. They led the league in innings pitched, strikeouts, and saves-holds. They assembled a pitching performance that would have won most games in most weeks of this season. They lost because the BANK of Texas had three starters finish in the top six of the weekly pitching leaderboard simultaneously — a convergence of individual excellence that the Indians’ reliable system was not built to overcome in a single week. The Indians are not a worse team for losing to the BANK in Week 3. They are the same team. Their 2-1 record is accurate.

I think often about the Tempe Scorpions this season. One thousand two hundred and seventy-one fantasy points in three weeks. Zero wins. Gavin Williams was the week’s second-best pitcher in a loss. Their construction — 764 cumulative hitting, 507 pitching — is not the problem. The schedule has been the problem. In head-to-head fantasy baseball, the schedule is not an abstraction. It is an opponent, and so far this season it has been Tempe Scorpions’ most formidable one. The talent exists. The calendar must cooperate.

Big Lou’s Corner: The Numbers Made Me Do It
By Lou Palmieri, Baseball Lifer  ·  The Longball Gazette

I need to start with the number that has been haunting me since Sunday night. Four. FOUR HOME RUNS. In a league where every home run is worth FOUR POINTS. Sidd Finch led the entire league with seventy-eight singles this week. SEVENTY-EIGHT. You know what the difference is between a single and a home run in this scoring system? Three points. Times seventy-four fewer singles you would need to match one home run if you had an average number of them instead of FOUR. I am not a mathematician. But I am a man who understands that leading the league in a category worth one point each while posting the league low in a category worth four points each is not a recipe for winning. Framber Valdez gave them 41.5 points and a 1.38 ERA and I want to formally apologize to Framber Valdez on behalf of his supporting cast.

THE CASCADES. FIVE HUNDRED POINTS. I said after Week 1 that the Cascades needed a closer and I have been saying it every week since then. This week they posted 50 SVH points. Is this the closer emerging? Is this circumstantial? I do not know. What I DO know is that a team generating 710 hitting points and 741 pitching points — the most balanced roster in the top three — with George Kirby (41.67, 2.02 ERA) and Mason Miller (39, 0.00 ERA) anchoring the staff is NOT a second-tier team. They are 137 points behind Goldy with two losses. They just posted the 2026 season high. If the closer situation is solved this team is frightening. Write that down.

The BANK of Texas. THREE PITCHERS IN THE TOP SIX. Cristopher Sanchez. Michael King. Bailey Ober. One hundred and twenty-seven point six-seven combined pitching points. For reference: that is more than Sidd Finch’s entire team produced from their pitching staff (106 pts). Three starters. One team. One week. Josh Jung goes ten for twenty-eight. Soto and Acuña have actual lineup support for the first time all season. FOUR HUNDRED AND SEVENTY-THREE POINTS. And they STILL LOST because Newberry Indians led the league in innings pitched AND strikeouts AND saves-holds on the same day. I am not making this up. I cannot make this up. This league is a masterpiece of competitive cruelty.

Will Warren of the Midland Red Weasel Dogs. Forty-six and a third points. Fourteen point three-four strikeouts per nine innings. ONE-POINT-SIX-NINE ERA. He is a weapon dressed as a starter. Paul Skenes added forty points at a zero-point-nine ERA on the same roster. TWO of the top eight pitchers in the league. Two hundred and eighty-two total team points. The hitting total is one hundred and eighty-five points — eleventh out of twelve teams. James Wood and Gunnar Henderson are better than that number. They need to start producing like it.

Desert Rats: 3-0. Weekly outputs: 528, 452, 325. Jeremiah Jackson — seasonal rank one hundred and eighteen, ownership seventy-two percent before this week — outscored Ohtani. Outscored Tucker. On the same roster. I said in February the Rats were the 2026 champions. I still believe it. But if Ohtani and Tucker don’t appear on a weekly top-five leaderboard before May I reserve the right to reconsider what exactly I believed about them.
