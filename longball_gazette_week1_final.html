Week 1 Final — March 25 through April 5, 2026
GOLDY RUNS AWAY WITH WEEK 1; WEASEL DOGS STEAL THE UPSET; RATS SURVIVE THEIR OWN CHAOS
Twelve days. Six matchups. One central truth: the teams that threw the most innings won. Goldy Goldy Gone posted a number that may not be topped all season. And the Desert Rats made 50 transactions to finish fifth in points scored. Progress.
By the Longball Gazette Wire Staff
League Headquarters — Monday, April 6, 2026

When the dust settled on Week 1 of the 2026 MLBLONGBALL season — the extended 12-day opening sprint from Opening Day through April 5 — only one number truly mattered: 738.5. That is what Goldy Goldy Gone posted. That is the highest single-week team total in recent league memory. That is what Max Fried and Kevin Gausman do when they are locked in and a team WHIP of 0.907 is not a typo but a way of life.

Goldy won. Sidd Finch won and turned heads doing it, posting 676 points on the back of Sandy Alcantara and Framber Valdez going full ace mode. Newberry Indians won by throwing 133 innings and outworking everyone in the building. The Desert Rats won, but it cost them fifty transactions and whatever credibility they had left after dropping the same catcher three times in five days. And in the week's defining upset, the Midland Red Weasel Dogs — who posted a sub-zero day, who gave up nine home runs, who had every right to be buried — clawed past the Houston Gamblers 518 to 498 on pitching volume and a Cam Schlittler performance that deserves its own parade.

The BANK of Texas, preseason darlings with Acuña and Soto locked in at a combined $2, got run off the field by the Troutdale Cascades. Ben Rice was spectacular. Everything else around him was a house fire with Juan Soto standing inside it, looking confused.

The Week's Central Truth: Innings Win

Every team that won in Week 1 threw more innings than their opponent. This is not coincidence — it is arithmetic. In a head-to-head points league where pitcher strikeouts earn 1.5 points, wins are worth 5, saves worth 5, and every earned run costs 2 points, volume is not just strategy. It is the game itself. Newberry's 133 innings. Goldy's 107. Finch's 122. The Cascades' 110. The lesson has been written in the final standings. Whether anyone reads it before Week 2 is another matter.

The Commissioner's Transaction Problem

The Desert Rats made over 50 transactions in 12 days. They cycled through catchers like rental cars, claimed and dropped Bailey Ober so many times the system may have flagged it as a glitch, and dropped Willy Adames only to win him back in a Vickrey auction the next morning. They won — beat the Ithaca Spartans 528 to 392 — and Freddie Freeman (43 pts), Kyle Tucker (36.5 pts), and Shohei Ohtani (33 pts) did the real work. But when your transaction log requires a separate document to summarize, something has gone wrong at the organizational level.

The Week That Rewrote the Script
By Joe Falls  ·  The Longball Gazette

You want to know what I saw this week? I saw Goldy Goldy Gone put up 738 points. Seven hundred and thirty-eight. I've been covering this league since 2008 and I don't know that I've seen a week like that from one team. Max Fried. Kevin Gausman. A team WHIP under 0.91. Ten saves. That's not a fantasy team. That's a pitching staff from a video game set to the wrong difficulty.

And then there's Sidd Finch. I wrote them off. You wrote them off. The preseason rankings had them twelfth and I didn't argue. They won by 208 points. Sandy Alcantara threw 16 innings, didn't give up an earned run, and scored 68.5 points. Drake Baldwin hit like a man with something to prove. Xavier Edwards batted .471. Whoever is managing Sidd Finch is doing something right and I'm not too proud to say so.

Now. The Desert Rats. They won — 528 to 392 — which is fine, except they made more roster moves than any team in league history in a single week. Fifty transactions. You know what that tells me? It tells me nobody was confident in anything. Freeman and Tucker and Ohtani are supposed to be the story. Joey Wiemer was their best hitter through the first five days. The keepers have since shown up — Freeman 43, Tucker 36.5, Ohtani 33 — and the Rats won anyway. But when your transaction log requires its own filing cabinet, you are playing the wrong kind of baseball.

The Ithaca Spartans need a serious conversation. Aaron Judge and Garrett Crochet. Twelfth place in the power rankings. 392 points. Chris Bassitt cost them 26.83 points. In this scoring system, a pitcher who gets shelled doesn't just not help you — he actively hurts you. The Spartans need to look at the bottom of that rotation and make some decisions that have nothing to do with name recognition.

The Midland Red Weasel Dogs won the week's best upset. They had a sub-zero day. They were outplayed offensively. They won on innings. Cam Schlittler was their best pitcher and he cost them practically nothing to acquire. CJ Abrams cost them a $510 Vickrey bid and one real dollar. That's the wire working. That's this game at its most interesting.

The Troutdale Cascades beat a team with Soto and Acuña on it. By 110 points. What does that tell you? It tells you that in this league, the pitching staff writes the story. The hitters just fill in the chapters.

What Twelve Days Taught Us
By Mitch Albom  ·  The Longball Gazette

There is a particular kind of humility that comes from being wrong about something you were certain of. The preseason rankings — published with confidence, placing Sidd Finch last and the Desert Rats first — have been met by twelve days of baseball with a polite but firm correction.

Consider what happened this week. The Troutdale Gales had the top individual hitter in the league through the opening days. They had Andy Pages posting a 1.294 OPS and William Contreras producing 43.5 points. They lost by 109 points. Not because their stars failed. Because on the mound, Roki Sasaki collected a total that a utility infielder on a rehab assignment would find embarrassing, and Brendon Little subtracted fifteen points from a roster that needed every point it could find.

There is a lesson here about the nature of team sports, even in their fantasy iteration. Excellence is not simply additive. You cannot have two elite hitters and call yourself an elite team. You cannot have one dominant ace and ignore the eight other roster spots. This game taxes completeness. It rewards the teams with no obvious holes, not the teams with the tallest peaks.

Goldy Goldy Gone understood this. Seven hundred and thirty-eight points was not the product of one Max Fried start — though it was extraordinary. It was the product of a roster assembled with this specific scoring system in mind. A 0.907 WHIP is not luck. Ten saves is not coincidence. It is construction.

I think often about Yordan Alvarez sitting in the Sons of Pitches dugout this week, posting 77.5 individual points — the most of any hitter in the league — and watching his team lose by 208. There is something almost Sisyphean about it. You do everything right. You hit the ball harder than anyone. And the scoreboard doesn't care, because baseball — real or fantasy — is played by rosters, not individuals.

The Houston Gamblers learned the same lesson differently. They won the hitting matchup against Midland. They lost the game because they gave Nathan Eovaldi a starting spot and generated zero saves from their bullpen in twelve days. Zero. That is not bad luck. That is a structural failure no amount of individual excellence can overcome.

Week 2 begins. The scores are reset. But the rosters are not. What was built in the draft, the Frenzy, and this opening week's transaction wire — that is what these twelve teams carry forward. Some of them are carrying something beautiful. Some are carrying something broken. The next seven days will tell us which is which.

Big Lou's Corner: I Was Right, I Was Wrong, and Midland Just Won a Game
By Lou Palmieri, Baseball Lifer  ·  The Longball Gazette

Alright. Let me get the record straight. In February I said the Desert Rats were your 2026 champions before anybody threw a pitch. I stand by the logic. What I did NOT account for was that the commissioner would spend the first week of the season treating the free agent wire like it was a yard sale and he had fifty dollars and forty-five minutes. FIFTY TRANSACTIONS. My friend, you have Shohei Ohtani on your roster. You do not need to claim and drop the same catcher three times in a week. You do not need to drop Willy Adames and then win him back in a Vickrey auction the next morning like you're bidding on yourself at a casino.

But I'll tell you what — they won. And that's the thing about chaos. Sometimes chaos works. Freeman forty-three points. Tucker thirty-six. Ohtani thirty-three. Dylan Cease shows up, Kyle Harrison shows up, and somehow through all the noise the Rats beat the Ithaca Spartans by 136 points. The Spartans, for their part, had Chris Bassitt cost them twenty-six points. I have no further comment on that situation.

Now let's talk about Goldy Goldy Gone because SEVEN HUNDRED AND THIRTY-EIGHT POINTS. That number should be on a trophy. Max Fried pitching a 1.35 ERA over twenty innings is what you draft a number-one ace for. Kevin Gausman going thirty innings at a 0.75 ERA is what you pray for. BOTH happening in the same week is what you call a masterpiece. The Tempe Scorpions actually outscored Goldy offensively. They lost by 160 points. That is not embarrassing. That is just getting in the way of something excellent.

Now. The part that is KILLING me. Sidd Finch is in second place. SECOND PLACE. The team I personally called a Ferrari with a shopping cart attached is sitting at 676 points and beat the Sons of Pitches by over two hundred. Sandy Alcantara pitched SIXTEEN INNINGS and did NOT GIVE UP AN EARNED RUN. Yordan Alvarez scored more points than anyone in the league for the Sons and they still lost by two hundred. That's not a loss. That's a philosophical statement about the inadequacy of individual excellence when your supporting cast is posting negative numbers.

I want to specifically acknowledge the Midland Red Weasel Dogs because they need to be called out properly. They had a negative point day. A day where the score went DOWN. They gave up nine home runs over the week. Shane Smith cost them twenty-five points. AND THEY WON. They won by twenty points against the Houston Gamblers because Cam Schlittler threw twelve innings of shutout baseball and CJ Abrams — acquired in a Vickrey auction where someone bid FIVE HUNDRED AND TEN DOLLARS and paid one — scored fifty-four points. That's not fantasy baseball. That's robbery with a boxscore.

The BANK of Texas lost. With Soto and Acuña. Two saves all week. Cristián Javier minus twenty-three. The Cascades beat them by a hundred and ten. I told you in February — pitching wins weeks in this league. The BANK has the names. They don't yet have the arms. Fix it, or February's promise becomes October's regret. And cut Griffin Jax, Tempe. That man owes your roster an apology.
