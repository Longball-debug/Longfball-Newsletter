Week 4 Final — April 20–26, 2026
THE UNDEFEATEDS DISAPPEAR; SOLER SAVES THE SPARTANS; WEASEL DOGS GO NUCLEAR
Week 4 erased every undefeated team in MLBLONGBALL in a single seven-day stretch. The Sons of Pitches slayed #1 Goldy. Sidd Finch crunched the sliding Desert Rats. The Ithaca Spartans — somehow, impossibly — won their first game of the season thanks to a Jorge Soler strikeout. Note to Newberry: Soler is your player.
By the Longball Gazette Wire Staff
League Headquarters — Sunday, April 26, 2026

For three weeks this league had undefeated teams. For three weeks the hierarchy felt established, the conversations were settled, and the preseason projections were holding up well enough that nobody felt the need to revise them. Then Week 4 arrived and in seven days of baseball it dismantled every one of those certainties, handed the last two undefeated franchises their first losses, and produced the most dramatic single game in recent MLBLONGBALL memory — a 382–381 extra-innings thriller decided by a strikeout from a player on the losing team.

The Sons of Pitches beat Goldy Goldy Gone 352 to 341. Let that settle for a moment. The same team that was blown out by 208 points in Week 1, that lost two consecutive games by a combined 20.5 points, that the league had essentially written off before May — that team walked into the #1 franchise's house and won by 11. Goldy's Max Fried delivered another zero-ERA outing. The offense around him went quiet. That is what happens when a team that has nothing to lose meets a team protecting something. The Sons had nothing to lose. Goldy had everything.

Meanwhile Sidd Finch put the Desert Rats away 376 to 310, extending what is now the most alarming trend line in the league. The Rats have declined 528, 452, 325, 310 — an average drop of 72 points per week for four consecutive scoring periods. Carlos Cortes, rank 125, a waiver claim, was once again the team's best hitter at 47.5 points. Shohei Ohtani's name does not appear on this week's leaderboard. The commissioner's best player and the commissioner's best hitter are currently two different people, and that is a sentence that was not supposed to be written in April.

The Soler Game — An Editorial Note

The Ithaca Spartans won their first game of the 2026 season, defeating the Newberry Indians 382.67 to 381.83. The margin was 0.84 points — the smallest in recent league history. The game went to extra innings. In the 9th inning, Jorge Soler came to the plate for the Newberry Indians and struck out, subtracting a point from Newberry's total. In the 10th, he struck out again. Two points lost. The Spartans won by less than one. The editorial staff of the Longball Gazette wishes to be absolutely clear: Jorge Soler plays for the Newberry Indians. The headline that read "Soler Lifts Spartans" was technically accurate in the most painful possible way. He lifted them by making Newberry's score worse. That is not how you want your keeper contributing in extra innings.

Weasel Dogs Rebound BIG — Season High

The Midland Red Weasel Dogs posted 486.83 points — the highest single-week total of the 2026 MLBLONGBALL season. They did it against the Troutdale Gales, a team that leads the league in cumulative hitting, winning by 187.66 points. Michael Harris II (48.5 pts), Sean Burke (36.67 pts, 1.35 ERA), and Paul Skenes (34 pts, 0.00 ERA) led the charge. The Cascades, meanwhile, continued their steady march toward contention with a 455-point dismantling of the winless Tempe Scorpions. The Gamblers rolled the Bankers 443.83 to 409.83 in the week's best two-good-team matchup. Week 4 belonged to the Dogs. The standings belong to three teams at 3-1.

⚡ ITHACA WINS BY 0.84 PTS  ·  EXTRA INNINGS  ·  SPARTANS FIRST WIN  ·  Ithaca: 1-3  ·  Newberry: 2-2
EDITOR’S NOTE: Jorge Soler plays for the Newberry Indians. He did not “lift” the Spartans in the traditional sense. He lifted them by striking out twice in extra innings while wearing Newberry's colors. This is fantasy baseball at its most elegant and most merciless.

The Ithaca Spartans were 0-3. They had the lowest cumulative point total in MLBLONGBALL. They had 382 cumulative pitching points across three weeks — dead last by 86 points. Nobody in this league expected them to win a game in April. Nobody except, apparently, the ghost of Jorge Soler's at-bat selection, which had other plans for the Newberry Indians.

This game was one of the great competitive matchups in recent MLBLONGBALL history. Justin Wrobleski (48.5 pts, 13 IP, 0.69 ERA) pitched the week's best game for Newberry. Max Muncy contributed 42.5 points. The Indians were excellent across the lineup. They deserved to win. Then came the 9th inning, point differential down to fractions, and Soler — a Newberry Indian — struck out. One point gone. Extra innings. Soler stepped up again in the 10th. Struck out again. Another point surrendered to the void. The Ithaca Spartans, who had done nothing wrong except exist in the same scoring period as this at-bat sequence, won by 0.84 points. Landen Roupp (44.83 pts, 12.2 IP, 2.84 ERA) was their pitching anchor. Josh Naylor (41 pts, 8.20 FP/G) was their offensive one.

The Spartans are 1-3. The road back is still long. But somewhere in Ithaca, a cold beer was poured for Jorge Soler, who will never know how much he contributed to someone else's first win of the season.

For three weeks the Goldy Goldy Gone machine ran without a hiccup. Seven hundred and thirty-eight points in Week 1. The league's highest FP/G. A win streak that made them look not just beatable but bulletproof. Then the Sons of Pitches showed up, posted 352 points, and handed the #1 team in MLBLONGBALL their first loss of the season by 11 points.

Max Fried was excellent for Goldy — 37 points, 8 innings, 0.00 ERA. The ace did his job. The lineup around him went quiet, producing the team's lowest weekly total of the 2026 season. The Sons, meanwhile, ran their lineup cleanly, stayed disciplined, and refused to do what everyone expected them to do, which was wilt in the shadow of the league's best franchise. Josh Naylor (41 pts) led the Sons offensively. They had beaten teams before. This time they beat the right one.

Context matters here. In Week 1, Goldy beat the Sons by 208 points. In Week 4, the Sons beat Goldy by 11. That swing — 219 points across four weeks — is the story of how a 2-2 team with a functioning identity is more dangerous than a 1-2 team still figuring itself out. The Sons have figured it out. Week 5 at home vs. the Desert Rats is their chance to prove it wasn't a one-week anomaly.

The ceiling is real. The question is whether the hitting — ninth in cumulative totals — can consistently match the pitching.

The Week Everything Changed
By Joe Falls  ·  The Longball Gazette  ·  J.G. Taylor Spink Award, 2001

I want to start with Jorge Soler because Jorge Soler is why I'm still at my desk at midnight on a Sunday. Not because he struck out — strikeouts happen, they are a documented feature of professional baseball — but because of when he struck out. The ninth inning of a game decided by less than one point. A Newberry Indians player, at the plate for the Newberry Indians, in a game the Newberry Indians needed, going down looking. And then the game going to extras. And him going down looking again. Two points subtracted. The Ithaca Spartans winning by 0.84.

I have been in this business for decades. I have watched Detroit teams lose games on wild pitches in the ninth inning. I have watched blown saves and missed cutoff throws and outfielders running into each other. None of it prepared me for losing a fantasy baseball game because your own player struck out at the most consequential moment of the season while wearing your uniform. The Newberry Indians are a good team. Justin Wrobleski gave them 48.5 points at 0.69 ERA. They did everything right for six days. Then Sunday happened.

The Sons of Pitches beat Goldy Goldy Gone. I want to repeat that because I wrote them off in Week 1 when Sidd Finch beat them by 208 points and they looked like a team that was attending the league instead of competing in it. They went into the #1 team's house and won by 11. Max Fried pitched a shutout for Goldy and the team still lost. That is what happens when you have an off week as the top team and the underdog has nothing to lose. Credit the Sons. They played better baseball.

The Desert Rats are 3-1 with a declining trend that now reads 528, 452, 325, 310. That is not a slump. A slump goes down and comes back up. That line has one direction. Carlos Cortes — rank 125, a man who costs nothing, a man whose name was not in anyone's preseason projection — is the Rats' best hitter two consecutive weeks. Shohei Ohtani is on this roster. I am not going to tell the commissioner what to do. I covered Detroit sports for thirty years and learned that unsolicited roster advice is the most ignored commodity in professional sports. I will simply note that Week 5 is at the Sons of Pitches, who just beat Goldy. That is the most dangerous game the Rats have faced. And they are arriving there at 310 points per week and falling.

Midland Red Weasel Dogs: 486.83 points. The season high. They beat the team that leads the league in cumulative hitting by 187 points. Michael Harris II. Sean Burke. Paul Skenes. These are real weapons and I was not taking them seriously enough. I am taking them seriously now. The Cascades are 3-1 and continue to dominate so quietly that I almost missed writing about them. They posted 455, Sal Stewart was the second-best hitter in the league, and they went home. No drama. No carousel. No 50 transactions. Just winning. The Tempe Scorpions are 0-4 with Chase Dollander pitching a 0.69 ERA in a blowout loss. I do not have the words for how unfair that is. I have some of the words. The words are: fix the schedule, give Dollander some run support, and stop making me watch the best pitcher on the worst team every single week.

Zero Point Eight Four
By Mitch Albom  ·  The Longball Gazette

There is a number at the center of this week's results that deserves more attention than a scoreboard can give it. Zero point eight four. That is how much the Ithaca Spartans won by on Sunday afternoon. Less than one point in a scoring system that distributes points across hundreds of individual moments — hits, strikeouts, earned runs, stolen bases, wins, losses — across seven days of baseball. The final margin came down to a fraction of one unit of one statistical category from one at-bat in extra innings.

What makes this number interesting is not its smallness. It is what produced it. Justin Wrobleski pitched thirteen innings at a 0.69 ERA for the Newberry Indians. That is elite, borderline-historic territory for a single week's performance. His team lost. Not because they played poorly — they led the league in pitching this week — but because their own player, at the plate in the ninth inning and again in the tenth, came back to the dugout without making contact. In a scoring system where each batter strikeout costs a point, Soler's two strikeouts were worth exactly the winning margin for the other team. This is not bad luck. This is roster construction operating at the margins of a precision instrument, and the margin decided the game.

I keep thinking about what the Newberry Indians must have felt when the final score was confirmed. They had done everything their model requires. Volume innings. Disciplined lineup management. Justin Wrobleski at 0.69 ERA. They were the better team by most metrics that matter. And the scoreboard said otherwise by less than a single stolen base.

The Sons of Pitches beating Goldy Goldy Gone tells a different kind of story — one about momentum and pressure and the way a team that has nothing to lose plays differently than a team that has everything to protect. The Sons lost by 208 points in Week 1. They have been building toward something since then. I don't know if Sunday's win was the destination or a waypoint. But it was real, and the 11-point margin over the league's best franchise is not something you explain away. Goldy had their worst week. The Sons had one of their better ones. Those two things happened simultaneously and the result was an upset.

The Desert Rats declining trend — 528, 452, 325, 310 — is a story about the distance between expectation and execution. This team was built around two elite keepers. Those keepers are not appearing in any weekly leaderboard. Carlos Cortes, rank 125, is doing their work for them. I write that without judgment. Sometimes that happens. The question is not whether it has happened — the data is clear — but whether it continues to happen long enough to change the season's outcome. Week 5 at Sons of Pitches is where we find out. The schedule is no longer protecting them.

Chase Dollander pitched 13 innings at a 0.69 ERA this week for the Tempe Scorpions. His team scored 279 points and lost by 176. I have no analytical framework for what Dollander is experiencing on that roster. I can only observe it and note that excellence, deployed in isolation, is not the same as winning. The Scorpions owe him more than they are currently providing.

Big Lou’s Corner: Soler, Streaks, and the Scorpion Situation
By Lou Palmieri, Baseball Lifer  ·  The Longball Gazette

JORGE SOLER. I need to say his name one more time because I have been saying it to myself since Sunday night and I don't think it's leaving my system anytime soon. Jorge Soler plays for the Newberry Indians. That is an established fact. He is on their roster. They chose him. They put him in the lineup. And in the ninth inning of a one-point game, he struck out. And then in the tenth inning, with the game still on the line, he came back up and struck out AGAIN. The Newberry Indians lost by zero point eight four points. To a team that was oh-and-three. The Newberry Indians have Justin Wrobleski throwing thirteen innings at a point-six-nine ERA this week — the best pitching performance in the league — and they lost because their own designated hitter decided the extra innings were a good time to start a strikeout streak.

I want to be fair to the Ithaca Spartans because they won and they deserve the recognition. Landen Roupp gave them 44.83 pitching points at a 2.84 ERA in twelve and two-thirds innings. Josh Naylor hit forty-one points at 8.20 per game. Those are real contributions. The Spartans are one-and-three and none of the math adds up to a good season yet. But they're not zero-and-four, and right now that matters more than anything else in Ithaca. That first win tastes different regardless of how it arrives. Even if it arrives gift-wrapped by the opposing roster.

THE SONS OF PITCHES BEAT GOLDY GOLDY GONE. I need everyone in this league to understand what that sentence means. Week ONE, Goldy beat the Sons by TWO HUNDRED AND EIGHT POINTS. Week FOUR, the Sons beat Goldy by eleven. That is a two-hundred-and-nineteen-point swing in the margin over one month of fantasy baseball. Either the Sons got dramatically better, or Goldy had their worst week of the season, or both. I think it was both. And I think the Sons beating the #1 team in the league is one of the better stories this league has produced in the four weeks I've been watching it.

The Desert Rats. Five-hundred-and-twenty-eight. Four-fifty-two. Three-twenty-five. Three-ten. I am going to say something that I said in February and I am going to say it again now: I believe in the Desert Rats. I believe Shohei Ohtani is a generational talent. I believe Kyle Tucker is an elite keeper. I believe Freddie Freeman is a professional. What I do NOT believe is that Carlos Cortes, rank one hundred and twenty-five, a man who cost the commissioner zero dollars and whose name was not mentioned in any preseason analysis ever written, should be the best hitter on that team for two consecutive weeks. That is a fact, not an opinion, and it is the most important fact in the Desert Rats' season right now. Week five is at the Sons of Pitches who just beat Goldy. The Rats are going to that game at three-hundred-and-ten points per week and declining. I am rooting for them. I am also nervous for them.

Midland Red Weasel Dogs: FOUR HUNDRED AND EIGHTY SIX POINTS. The season high. Michael Harris the Second. Sean Burke at a one-thirty-five ERA. Paul Skenes at zero ERA. They beat the team that leads the entire league in hitting by a hundred and eighty-seven points. I was sleeping on the Weasel Dogs. I am now fully awake. And Chase Dollander of the Tempe Scorpions pitched a zero-point-six-nine ERA in a hundred-and-seventy-six-point loss for a zero-and-four team that has the fifth-best hitting total in the league. I want everyone who runs this schedule to take a long look at what they've done to Tempe. That's all I'm going to say. That's everything.
